# Nier: Automata

- It is a creative use games as a storytelling medium (ending E for example).
- I loved the side quests. They help build a better understanding of the
    game world.
- Deals with dark and philosophical themes, while still managing to put in
    some humor (sometimes dark).
- Whether it is in battles, key moments, credits or just chilling around, the
    music in the game is so fucking good.

<div class="spoiler">

Let's begin with things I didn't like

- A2 could have used more screentime.
- Camera during battles could be more refined (locking in particular).
- Maps were awful.

There are so many things I loved about the game
- The use of credits in Ending E, where we are fighting the creators is genius and one of
    the most heartmoving moments in gaming. It also ties back to the game's
    opening line by 2B.
- 9S is my favorite character. How he breaks after knowing the truth of humanity
    and 2B's death was handled brilliantly by the story and the voice actor.

## Music and Sound
The music takes this epic game and propels it to the moon. The sound design does
a notable job of integrating the music into the game. I loved the smooth
transition to 8-bit soundtrack and back during hacking. "Weight of the world" is
used to its full potential. The heavy notes in the song justified the song
title.

## Notes
This has got me interested in exploring more existentialism literature.
Machines and Android are tied to the purpose given to them by the aliens and
humans respectively, their gods.  When the "God is dead", in both cases, how
does one find purpose? Machines disconnected from the network handle this by
imitating humans. Pascal sets up a village built around pacifism. Some machines
form the forest kingdom around a king.  At the abandoned factory, we see the
machines forming a religion and taking it to an extreme level. I loved the story
behind all these machines. Then there are individual machines, such as the
racing machine and Father Servo, who despite having a comedic tone, have found a
purpose. When they are left with no purpose they choose to destroy themselves.
Some individual machines are unable to find a purpose to begin with, and destroy
themselves. Adam and Eve take human imitation a step further. We see machines
taking human genders, family roles, enacting shakespeare's work, having fun in
amusement park, etc. Despite all this, machines always fail. Yet they never
learn and repeat the same mistakes.  This especially makes the decision we have
to make with Pascal towards the end a very hard one: Killing Pascal vs Wiping
his memory. While killing Pascal is something only a devil would do, wiping his
memory will only lead him to repeat his mistakes. There is a third less obvious
option which the game lets us do. Morally it might be the best option but also
heartbreaking.  We can leave the factory and let Pascal live with his memories.
Pascal might suffer but at least we leave the choice to him. There is a small
hope that he makes it out of this mess (althogh I believe it is suggested that
he kills himself instead). Wiping his memory also leads to a sad moment where we
find a clueless Pascal selling the parts of village children without knowing
where they came from.

We don't get to see similar attempts from androids since their purpose is built
on lies of humans being alive. For the androids who do knwo the truth, 9S goes
insane. A2 is hard to explain, but she does bear hatred towards machines and
inherits the will of 2B to save 9S. The pods have a purpose of serving their
assigned yorha units but in Ending E, create the own meaning and exercise their
free will even after their androids seem to have perished.
</div>

# Journey

- The game has a philosophical undertone and can be
    possibly interpreted in multiple ways. The story is open to interpretation
    and is in my opinion one of the key strengths.
- The game and score were made for each other. The ending brings out the best of
    the music, art and gameplay.
- <span class="spoiler">There are multiple interpretations to the game, which I
    believe is intentional. There is the in-game universe story of the
    rise, fall and rebirth of a civilization. Then there is the metaphorical
    interpretation of life, death, after-life and rebirth. Lastly, this is
    literally a reference to Campbell's Hero's Journey.</span>

# Finding Paradise (To The Moon 2)

- This series is easily among my all time favories
